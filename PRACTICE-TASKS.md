## How These Tasks Work

Each section has three layers:

1. **Theory bite** — just enough context to understand what you're doing
2. **Practice tasks** — hands-on micro-projects (not quizzes)
3. **Test-drive question** — would you do this daily?

Complete at least one task per section. If you enjoy it, keep going. If not, that's valuable data too.

## `S1` · HealthTech / Bio / Med

### Theory

HealthTech and biomedical work sits at the intersection of **evidence, regulation, and human impact**. Key concepts:

- **Evidence hierarchy**: systematic reviews > RCTs > cohort studies > case reports > expert opinion
- **Regulatory reality**: medical devices and drugs require clinical trials, FDA/EMA approval, and post-market surveillance
- **Translation gap**: it takes 10-17 years from lab discovery to clinical use
- **Ethics**: informed consent, data privacy (HIPAA/GDPR), equity in access

Daily reality: lots of literature review, protocol writing, data analysis, and navigating bureaucracy. The payoff: your work can literally save lives.

### Practice Tasks

1. **Glossary builder** — Pick one sub-area (e.g., wearable diagnostics, telemedicine, genomics). Create a plain-language glossary for 10 key terms. Each entry: term, definition (1 sentence), why it matters (1 sentence).

2. **Resource comparison** — Find 3 public resources for beginners in your sub-area (e.g., NCBI, PubMed, Khan Academy medicine). Compare them on: depth, accessibility, update frequency, credibility. Recommend one for a complete beginner and explain why.

3. **Problem map** — Draft a one-page problem map for a science-informed MVP idea:
   - What is the health problem?
   - Who is affected?
   - What current solutions exist?
   - Where is the gap?
   - What could technology do?

4. **Evidence rating** — Find 3 health claims on social media or news. For each, trace the claim to its source and rate the evidence quality using the hierarchy above. How many hold up?

5. **Regulatory obstacle course** — Pick a hypothetical health product (e.g., an AI skin-cancer screening app). List the regulatory steps needed to bring it to market in your region. How long would each step take?

### Test-Drive Question

Could you spend 8 hours a day reading papers, writing protocols, and navigating regulatory processes — knowing that your work might take years to reach patients?

## `S2` · Green / Sustainability

### Theory

Sustainability science is about **understanding Earth systems and finding leverage points for change**. Key concepts:

- **Planetary boundaries**: 9 Earth-system processes with quantified boundaries (climate, biodiversity, freshwater, etc.)
- **Carbon accounting**: Scope 1 (direct), Scope 2 (energy), Scope 3 (supply chain) emissions
- **Life cycle assessment (LCA)**: total environmental impact of a product from cradle to grave
- **Systems thinking**: interventions in one area ripple through others (e.g., biofuels reduce carbon but increase land use)

Daily reality: data analysis, policy writing, stakeholder negotiation, and managing the tension between scientific urgency and political/economic reality.

### Practice Tasks

1. **Problem → actors → interventions** — Map one climate or sustainability problem into this structure. Be specific: name the actors, quantify the interventions where possible.

2. **Resource ladder** — Build a "what to read first / second / later" list for someone new to sustainability science. Include 5 resources, each with a 1-sentence summary and why it's at that level.

3. **Sphere classification** — Write a mini note: "Where does this problem become S, E, T, or S+E+T?" Pick one sustainability challenge and show how different approaches fall into different SET combinations.

4. **Carbon math** — Calculate the approximate carbon footprint of one daily activity (e.g., your commute, your lunch, your internet use). Show your assumptions and sources.

5. **Policy brief** — Write a 1-page brief recommending one sustainability policy to a local government. Include: the problem (with data), the policy, expected impact, and one counterargument with your rebuttal.

### Test-Drive Question

Can you handle working on problems where progress is measured in decades, not sprints — and where the stakes are existential?

## `S3` · AgTech & Food Security

### Theory

Agricultural technology and food security combine **biology, logistics, data science, and policy**. Key concepts:

- **Food system stages**: production → processing → distribution → retail → consumption → waste
- **Food security dimensions** (FAO): availability, access, utilization, stability
- **Precision agriculture**: using sensors, drones, and data to optimize inputs (water, fertilizer, pesticides)
- **Post-harvest loss**: 30-40% of food produced is never eaten — the biggest leverage point is often not production but distribution

Daily reality: field work, supply chain analysis, data collection, and bridging the gap between farmers and technologists.

### Practice Tasks

1. **Comparison table** — Create a short comparison of AgriTech, FoodTech, and Agri-data systems. For each: definition, key technologies, main challenges, and one example company/project.

2. **Starter project brief** — Draft a one-page brief for a learner who wants to start an AgTech project. Include: problem statement, technology approach, required skills, and a 30-day plan.

3. **Evaluation checklist** — Create a visual checklist for evaluating an agricultural problem. Criteria: is it a production problem or distribution problem? Is the solution technical or social? What data would you need?

4. **Supply chain trace** — Pick one food item and trace it from farm to table. At each stage, identify: where waste occurs, where data is missing, and where technology could help.

5. **Yield vs. waste analysis** — Research: for one crop in your region, compare the theoretical yield vs. actual yield vs. what reaches consumers. Where is the biggest gap?

### Test-Drive Question

Can you work on problems where the "users" are farmers, ecosystems, and supply chains — and where results are measured in harvests, not release cycles?

## `S4` · Human Systems / Learning

### Theory

Learning science and human systems study **how people acquire, retain, and apply knowledge**. Key concepts:

- **Cognitive load theory**: working memory is limited — instruction should reduce unnecessary load
- **Spaced repetition**: memory decays exponentially; review at increasing intervals to flatten the curve
- **Deliberate practice**: focused practice with feedback beats mindless repetition
- **Transfer learning**: applying knowledge from one domain to another is hard and must be explicitly practiced
- **Metacognition**: thinking about your own thinking — the strongest predictor of learning success

Daily reality: designing learning experiences, analyzing what works, iterating based on feedback, and fighting the "just add more content" instinct.

### Practice Tasks

1. **7-day self-tracking challenge** — Design a template for a 7-day learning experiment. Track: what you studied, how long, what method, and how much you retained (self-rated). After 7 days, analyze: what worked best?

2. **Research-to-beginner note** — Take one research-facing topic (e.g., spaced repetition, cognitive load, growth mindset) and summarize it in a beginner-friendly note. Include: the finding, why it matters, and one practical tip.

3. **Anti-chaos guide** — Draft a "how to learn this without chaos" guide for one subject you know well. Structure: 5 most important concepts, recommended order, common traps, and one output to aim for.

4. **Curriculum audit** — Find one online course or tutorial. Evaluate it against learning science principles: does it space practice? provide feedback? build incrementally? Write 3 specific improvements.

5. **Teach-back exercise** — Explain one concept you recently learned to an imaginary 12-year-old. If you can't simplify it, you don't understand it yet. Identify where you got stuck.

### Test-Drive Question

Do you find yourself naturally thinking about how to make knowledge accessible to others — and could you do that as your daily work?
